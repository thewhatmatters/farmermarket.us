Spring officially arrived this week — and farmers markets across the country are feeling it. Markets are expanding their footprints, vendor waitlists are overflowing, and the first real spring crops are making their appearances. Here's what happened in local food this week.

---

## Ames Farmers Market Expands to Meet Record Demand

One of this week's biggest stories comes from Ames, Iowa, where the Main Street Farmers' Market announced it's expanding into the 200 block of Main Street for the 2026 season.

The reason? Demand. Last season, the market topped **8,000 visitors on a single market day** — and a long-standing vendor waiting list made it clear that the original footprint could no longer contain the community's appetite for local food.

"The expansion reflects the continued momentum and popularity of the market, which has become a weekly destination for local food, artisan goods, and community connection," market organizers said in a statement.

Ames isn't alone. Across the country, markets are responding to the same pressure — more vendors wanting in, more shoppers showing up, and communities increasingly treating their Saturday market as a weekly anchor event rather than an occasional outing.

---

## Vendor Applications Are Surging Everywhere

It's not just Ames. Market organizers nationwide are reporting a surge in vendor applications heading into spring 2026.

The Standard-Examiner spoke with organizers who noted the pattern is tied to two converging forces: economic uncertainty pushing more small producers toward direct-to-consumer sales, and a shopper base that's actively seeking alternatives to grocery store inflation.

"It's partly a reflection of the economic times as well as a quest for healthier food," one organizer explained.

Massachusetts alone has published a list of over a dozen markets actively seeking vendors for the 2026 season, from spring openings in April through summer markets running into October. The West Windsor Farmers Market in New Jersey is finishing its winter schedule before shifting to weekly outdoor Saturdays starting May 2 — and is already prepping for capacity.

---

## Strawberries Are Almost Here (If the Frost Cooperates)

The Pepper Place Market in Birmingham, Alabama, put out its monthly farmer forecast this week — and it's all about strawberries.

"Late winter frosts determine when strawberry season begins," they wrote, noting they've been checking in directly with their strawberry growers about the 2026 crop. Pepperplace is one of the country's most well-regarded year-round farmers markets, and when they're focused on strawberries, it's a signal that the broader Southeast season is close.

For shoppers in colder regions, strawberries are still weeks out — but if you're in the Southeast or Texas, start checking your local market now. Barton Creek Farmers Market in Austin already lists fresh strawberries alongside asparagus, carrots, garlic, and new potatoes as current spring offerings.

---

## Asparagus: In Season, But Tight

This week's produce markets report is a little bittersweet for asparagus fans. Primo Produce's March 20 market report notes that **asparagus supplies are tight** — Mexican production is running well below normal heading into Easter, and extreme heat forecasts could add further pressure through early April.

The good news: asparagus *is* at markets. The challenge is price and availability — smaller sizes are more common right now, and larger grades are harder to find. If your market vendor has it, don't wait.

For context on what to look for: firm stalks, tight tips, and a clean snap when bent. Whether thick or thin, freshness matters more than size. And if you're near an Alabama or Georgia market, local-grown asparagus is starting to appear as an alternative to the Mexican imports that dominate retail.

---

## Spring Has Officially Arrived at Markets

The Charlottesville Farmers Market at Ix captured the mood well in their weekly update this week:

*"Spring has officially arrived at the market. The spaces are filling up, and the farmers are bringing greens and garden plants."*

They noted it's still too early for basil, tomatoes, and peppers — but hardy herbs, overwintered greens, and garden starts are filling the stalls. The Trail Farmers Market in British Columbia returned for its spring season on March 21 with live music and community events. Kansas City rounded up eight local markets for the metro area's spring kickoff.

The rhythm of the season is back.

---

## What to Buy This Weekend

If you're heading to your local market this weekend, here's what to prioritize:

- **Asparagus** — Buy it when you see it. Supplies are tight and prices may climb toward Easter.
- **Spring greens** — Spinach, arugula, and tender lettuces are at peak right now.
- **Radishes** — Abundant, affordable, and underrated. Slice thin on buttered bread with flaky salt.
- **Green onions and garlic scapes** — Perfect for brightening spring dishes.
- **Hardy herbs** — Chives, thyme, and parsley are market staples right now. Skip the grocery store versions.
- **Strawberries** *(if you're in the South)* — Check your local market. Season is opening.

---

## The Bigger Picture

This week's market news adds up to a clear signal: **the local food movement is outgrowing its infrastructure**. Markets are expanding their physical footprints. Vendor waitlists are overflowing. Attendance records are being broken. Federal funding ($26.8 million from USDA, announced March 10) is flowing into the system.

For shoppers, this is good news — more vendors, more variety, more access. For the markets themselves, it's a welcome challenge: how do you grow without losing the community feel that makes a farmers market different from any other shopping experience?

The markets expanding right now seem to understand the answer: you grow carefully, you preserve the gathering-place quality, and you keep the farmer-shopper relationship at the center of everything.
