Something is shifting at farmers markets this spring — and you can feel it in the crowd. What used to be a Saturday ritual for food enthusiasts has quietly become a strategic decision for families watching their grocery budgets. More people are arriving with meal plans already drafted, vendor lists pulled up on their phones, and a clear intention to shop local not just once, but every week.

This isn't a niche trend. A recent survey by Trace One found that **42.9% of Americans now rank farmers markets as their second-most-used source for local food**, right behind supermarkets. More telling: market managers across the country report that first-time visitors are increasingly becoming regulars — and that those regulars are shifting their shopping, not supplementing it.

Here's why farmers markets have become the smartest move you can make for your food budget this spring.

---

## The Tariff Effect: Why Local Suddenly Makes More Economic Sense

The global food system is under pressure. Ongoing trade tensions have sent ripples through supply chains for everything from fertilizer to imported produce. Shoppers who used to reach for Chilean blueberries in February or Mexican tomatoes in March are now asking a different question: *What's grown near me?*

When food travels thousands of miles, it carries the cost of every tariff, fuel surcharge, and supply chain disruption along the way. Local food doesn't. A head of lettuce grown forty miles away doesn't care about port congestion or currency fluctuations.

"Shorter supply chains mean fewer points of failure," explains Dr. Bill Schindler, anthropologist and author of *Eat Like a Human*. "For us, that connection strengthens not just flavor and nutrition, but the resilience of our community as a whole."

The economic upside extends to farmers too. At a farmers market, producers take home approximately **90 cents on every dollar** — compared to around 15 cents when selling through supermarkets. That margin makes a real difference for small farms, and it means the people growing your food have a sustainable reason to keep growing it.

## $26.8 Million in New USDA Support

On March 10th, the USDA made its commitment to local food systems concrete: **$26.8 million in new grants** through the Local Agriculture Market Program (LAMP). The funding supports three programs:

- **Farmers Market Promotion Program (FMPP)**: $11.1 million to 43 projects across 28 states, funding direct producer-to-consumer markets including farmers markets, CSAs, and agritourism.
- **Local Food Promotion Program (LFPP)**: $11.1 million to 37 projects across 24 states, supporting food businesses that aggregate and distribute local products.
- **Regional Food System Partnerships (RFSP)**: $4.7 million across 10 states to bring local food into schools, hospitals, and government meal programs.

"This funding is putting Farmers First by helping them connect with new markets, strengthening their businesses, and boosting economic resilience in rural communities," said Agriculture Secretary Brooke L. Rollins.

For shoppers, this means better-resourced markets, more consistent vendor participation, and expanded infrastructure for the local food systems they're increasingly depending on.

## Your SNAP Benefits Go Further at the Farmers Market

One of the most underused advantages of farmers market shopping is what happens when you pay with SNAP benefits.

Thousands of markets across the country now accept Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT), and many of them participate in matching programs that effectively **double — or triple — your purchasing power**. Programs like Double Up Food Bucks match every SNAP dollar spent on produce, meaning a $10 EBT purchase becomes $20 worth of fresh fruits and vegetables.

In Washington State, SNAP Market Match benefits increased to $10 or more per day starting January 1, 2026. In California, Governor Newsom's office announced $23 million in new funding in March to expand farm-fresh food access for CalFresh, WIC, and Senior Farmers' Market Nutrition Program recipients.

The USDA's Farmers Market Directory (searchable at apps.ams.usda.gov) lets you filter markets by accepted payment types — including SNAP/EBT — so you can find participating markets near you before you leave home.

## The "Primary Stop" Shift

What's changing most noticeably this spring is the *intention* shoppers bring to the market.

"We're seeing more intentional local food buying and more customers treating the farmers market as a primary weekly shopping stop — not just a special trip," says Paula Stocker, market manager at City Market in Kansas City. "Customer turnout continues to grow across families and younger shoppers, with people looking for fresh food, direct connections with growers, and a more social shopping experience."

The math supports this shift. In-season produce at a farmers market is often **cheaper than the same item at a grocery store**, because it doesn't carry the costs of long-haul transport and cold storage. Chef Sam Diminich of Your Farms Your Table puts it plainly: "There's a common misconception that 'farm-to-table' is more expensive, but I've learned that it's quite the opposite. When you cook seasonally, you're not paying the premium for out-of-season product."

Farmers markets have also evolved far beyond fruits and vegetables. Today's markets offer local honey, artisan cheeses, pastured meats, eggs, bread, fermented foods, and specialty items that can replace several different grocery store trips. Cooperative sourcing — where farms aggregate products from neighboring producers — means some market stands function as a genuine one-stop shop.

## What to Expect at Your Market This Week

If you're reopening your routine or visiting for the first time this season, here's what to look for right now across US markets:

- **Asparagus** — first significant spring crop in most regions, usually hits stands mid-March through May
- **Spring greens** — arugula, spinach, mâche, and loose-leaf lettuce from cold-frame and tunnel growers
- **Radishes and turnips** — fast-growing root vegetables that appear early
- **Fresh herbs** — chives, parsley, and mint are reliable March finds
- **Storage staples from local farms** — winter squash, potatoes, and cured onions still available from last year's harvest

Many markets now post weekly vendor lists on their websites or social media the evening before market day. Checking ahead lets you arrive with a plan, know who'll be there, and skip the guesswork entirely.

## The Community Dividend

There's an economic case for farmers market shopping that goes beyond the stall. Research from the University of Wisconsin found that **nearly half of farmers market shoppers visit other local businesses on the same trip** — a compounding effect that keeps money circulating in the community.

"It keeps dollars circulating within our local economy," says Sean Blomgren, executive chef of The Lodge at Spruce Peak in Vermont. "When we buy directly from farmers and producers, we're reinvesting in our community and strengthening long-term partnerships."

That's a return that doesn't show up on a grocery receipt — but it shows up in the businesses, farms, and community spaces that survive and thrive around a healthy local food system.
