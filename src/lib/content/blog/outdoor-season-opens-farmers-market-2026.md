The tents are going up. The vendor lists are growing. And across the country, the 2026 outdoor farmers market season is arriving earlier, bigger, and with more to offer than any recent year.

This isn't a quiet reopening. Markets from Ames, Iowa to Bentonville, Arkansas are announcing expansions. Vendors who spent years on waiting lists are finally getting their spots. And shoppers — more of them than ever — are circling Saturday mornings on their calendars not as a nice-to-have, but as a weekly routine.

If you haven't been to your local market in a while, here's what's changed.

---

## Markets Are Getting Bigger to Keep Up With Demand

The most striking development this spring is simple: markets are running out of room.

In Ames, Iowa, the Ames Main Street Farmers' Market is expanding into the 200 block of Main Street for the 2026 season — a direct response to surging demand. Last season, more than 8,000 people turned up on a single market day. The expansion will accommodate approximately 95 vendors each week, up from previous seasons, and gives a chance to vendors who have been on the waiting list for years.

"The Ames Main Street Farmers' Market has grown into one of the most popular gatherings in our community," said Market Manager Lojean Petersen. "Expanding into the 200 block allows us to welcome more vendors and create an even better experience for the thousands of people who come downtown on Saturday mornings."

That pattern is repeating across the country. The Adams County Farmers Market in Gettysburg, Pennsylvania capped 2025 with its most successful year ever — combined vendor sales exceeding $750,000 for the first time in market history. Market Director Reza Djalal is already planning more themed market days and a larger vendor roster for 2026, with an eye on America's 250th birthday celebrations as a centerpiece event.

The Port Austin Farmers Market in Michigan is adding three extra weeks to its calendar. The Downtown Calhoun Farmers Market in Georgia is opening earlier than ever. The West Windsor Farmers Market in New Jersey begins outdoor operations on May 2, with a full weekly Saturday schedule through fall.

The outdoor season, in short, is bigger this year by design — and by necessity.

---

## Vendor Diversity Has Expanded Beyond Recognition

Ten years ago, "farmers market" meant produce stands, maybe some honey and jam. That era is over.

Today's most popular markets are full-service food destinations. The Ann Arbor Farmers Market, one of the country's oldest, now features 130 Michigan-only vendors offering everything from fresh lake fish and pastured meats to microgreens, gourmet mushrooms, artisan cheese, sourdough bread, pickles, hot sauce, and gluten-free pastries.

At the Adams County market, 2025 brought vendors like Grace's Family Farm — offering rabbit, goat, turkey, and hard cheeses made from their own herd. Another newcomer, Low Carb Avenue, sells keto-friendly, gluten-free breads, serving customers who've long felt left out of the carb-heavy farmers market experience.

"We had some really cool new additions this year," said Market Director Djalal. "There's a niche for everything now. Keto breads, specialty mushrooms, heritage-breed meats — the diversity is remarkable."

This vendor evolution is reshaping why people come to markets. It's no longer about topping off a grocery run with some fresh tomatoes. Increasingly, it's about replacing the grocery run entirely.

"One prominent trend in local food is the increase of cooperative marketing," explains Devin Cornia, executive director at Northeast Organic Farming Association of NJ. "You'll see vegetable farmers partnering with local grains growers, livestock farmers and other businesses to fill up their farm stores and market stands, giving customers a one-stop shopping alternative to the supermarket."

## Opening Weekend: What to Expect at the Stalls Right Now

For markets opening in mid-to-late March and April, here's the spring produce reality check — what's actually there, and what's still a few weeks out:

**What's at the market now:**

- **Asparagus** — The first big spring crop in most of the country. Look for tight tips and firm stalks. This window closes faster than you think.
- **Radishes** — Spring radishes are sweeter and more tender than summer varieties. French breakfast radishes, watermelon radishes, and daikon are all worth grabbing.
- **Overwintered greens** — Kale, spinach, and arugula that made it through the cold are genuinely sweeter than their summer counterparts. Don't overlook them.
- **Rhubarb** — Early arrivals from tunnel growers and warmer climates. Peak baking season.
- **Spring onions and alliums** — Green garlic, chives, and spring onions are showing up in abundance.
- **Fresh herbs** — Parsley, cilantro, and mint are reliable this time of year. Worth loading up on.

**What's coming:**
- Strawberries (slight delays in some regions due to late frosts, but arriving by April in warmer zones)
- Snap peas and sugar snap peas
- New potatoes
- Artichokes from California growers

The key this time of year: go with flexibility. "The biggest thing I tell people is to shop by season, not by recipe," says Shruthi Baskaran-Makanju of Urban Farmie. "If you know what's in season right now, you'll cook better food and waste less of it."

## The Community Is Showing Up in New Ways

The practical case for farmers markets — fresh food, value, local economy — is well established. What's newer is the social function markets are serving in 2026.

A March 2025 University of Wisconsin study found that **80% of Americans now visit a farmers market at least once a year**, and 41% attend six or more times annually. Significantly, the study noted that socializing and learning are now major draws — not just the food itself.

"We're seeing more intentional local food buying and more customers treating the farmers market as a primary weekly shopping stop," says Paula Stocker, market manager at City Market in Kansas City. "Customer turnout continues to grow across families and younger shoppers, with people looking for fresh food, direct connections with growers and a more social shopping experience."

Markets have responded by building more programming around that social dimension. Themed market days, cooking demonstrations, kids activities, live music, and meet-the-farmer events are all increasing. At the Adams County market, Kids Day — the first Saturday in August — drew their biggest crowds of the year. "There are games, prizes, and fun ways for kids to try new fruits and vegetables," said Djalal. "It's a beautiful, community-focused day."

Markets that have added these events report that they boost vendor sales and bring in first-time visitors who become regulars.

## Food Access Programs Are Expanding

One of the more significant developments across markets this year: the growth of food access programs that make the market work for everyone, not just those who can pay full price.

The Adams County market administered $128,000 in food program benefits in 2025 — up $30,000 from 2024. The market doubles every SNAP dollar spent. "If someone spends $20 in SNAP benefits, we give them $20 more," said Djalal. "It makes a huge difference."

That model — matching EBT benefits with additional market dollars — is replicating across the country. In Washington State, SNAP Market Match benefits increased to $10 or more per day starting January 1, 2026. The Western Sustainability Exchange, which runs the Livingston Farmers Market in Montana, announced it would hold vendor fees flat for 2026 thanks to community fundraising — keeping the market accessible to small farms that can't absorb rising costs.

"The effects of shutting down SNAP recently have actually brought the advantages of farmers markets to the attention of folks who weren't aware that many markets match benefits," notes Catt Fields White, CEO of Farmers Market Pro. "Markets and their shoppers contributed funds to be sure their community members didn't go hungry. That brought more people to markets, and they've stayed."

## How to Make the Most of the 2026 Season

Whether you're a first-timer or a longtime regular, here's how to get more from the outdoor season that's just getting started:

**1. Go early — especially in spring.** First-of-season produce moves fast. Asparagus and rhubarb wait for no one.

**2. Talk to your vendors.** Ask which farm the food came from, when it was harvested, how they grow it. These conversations are the whole point of a market. Farmers who share that information are doing something grocery stores fundamentally cannot.

**3. Look beyond the produce.** Explore the mushroom vendor, the specialty bread baker, the fermented foods stand. Markets have become full food ecosystems — use them that way.

**4. Check for SNAP matching programs.** If you use EBT, look up whether your local market participates in a matching program before you go. Thousands of markets now double your purchasing power on fresh produce.

**5. Mark your calendar for themed days.** Opening days, harvest festivals, kids days, and seasonal events are when markets bring their best energy — and often their rarest vendors.
