Spring greens are hitting farmers markets across the country, and while a simple salad with lemon and olive oil is delicious, there's a whole world of possibilities waiting in those tender leaves. Whether you're a market regular or it's your first spring visit, here's how to transform those delicate greens into something extraordinary.

![Fresh spring peas and greens at the farmers market](/images/blog/2026-02/peas-spring.jpg)

## The Spring Greens You’ll Find in March

**Baby Spinach** — Not to be confused with the mature spinach that arrives later in the season, baby spinach leaves are tender, sweet, and perfect for quick cooking. Look for bright green leaves without any yellowing or wilting.

**Arugula** — The peppery star of spring, arugula gets sweeter as temperatures warm. March arugula is milder than the summer version, making it perfect for those new to the green.

**Mâche (Lamb’s Lettuce)** — This delicate little green has a nutty, slightly sweet flavor that pairs beautifully with fruits, cheeses, and nuts. It's a chef's favorite but remains surprisingly underutilized by home cooks.

**Butter Lettuce & Bibb** — Creamy, tender, and buttery in texture (hence the name), these lettuces are the basis for some of the world's most beloved salads.

**Spring Onions & Green Garlic** — Not technically greens, but these spring alliums are essential for cooking with green vegetables. Use the entire plant — bulbs, stems, and those beautiful green tops.

**Microgreens & Sprouts** — These tiny powerhouses are bursting with flavor and nutrition. From radish microgreens to pea shoots, they add visual appeal and concentrated taste to any dish.

## Beyond the Salad Bowl

### Wilted Greens with Garlic & Lemon

The simplest preparation might be the most transformative. Heat olive oil in a pan, add minced garlic (or green garlic), and toss in washed greens just until wilted. Finish with a squeeze of fresh lemon and a pinch of salt. This takes about 90 seconds and works with any spring green.

### Spring Green Pesto

Toss the classic basil for a spring-inspired pesto:

- 2 cups spring greens (arugula, spinach, or mâche)
- 1/2 cup nuts (walnuts, almonds, or pine nuts)
- 1/2 cup parmesan, grated
- 2 cloves garlic (or 3-4 stems green garlic)
- 1/2 cup olive oil
- Salt and pepper to taste

Blend everything until smooth. This pesto works on pasta, as a sandwich spread, or drizzled over roasted vegetables.

### Green Goddess Dressing

This vibrant dressing is having a moment, and there's a reason — it's absolutely delicious on everything from grain bowls to roasted chicken.

- 1 cup mixed spring greens
- 1/2 cup parsley
- 1/4 cup chives
- 1 avocado
- 2 tablespoons lemon juice
- 1 clove garlic
- 1/2 cup olive oil
- Salt to taste

Blend until creamy. Adjust consistency with water as needed.

### Spring Greens Frittata

Whisk 6 eggs with salt, pepper, and a splash of milk. Sauté spring onions and garlic in an oven-safe skillet, add torn greens, pour in the egg mixture, and cook on the stovetop until the edges set. Finish under the broiler for a fluffy, golden top.

### Spring Green Soup

Blanch spinach or other tender greens, blend with vegetable broth, add a splash of cream (optional), and season with nutmeg. This elegant soup comes together in minutes and makes an impressive appetizer.

## Meet Your Farmer: Why Local Matters

The greens at your farmers market are fundamentally different from what you'll find at the grocery store. Here's why:

**Picked That Morning** — Grocery store greens can spend 5-14 days in transit. Market greens? Often harvested the morning of or night before.

**Variety You Can’t Find Elsewhere** — Farmers grow unique varieties specifically for farmers markets — baby lettuces, specialty arugulas, and heirloom greens you'll never see on a supermarket shelf.

**Reduced Food Miles** — Your $5 bunch of spinach didn't cross an ocean or travel across the country. It came from a farm within 50 miles of your market.

**Supporting Local Agriculture** — Every purchase supports a local farm and keeps agricultural land in production in your community.

## Tips for Buying Spring Greens at the Market

**Go Early for the Best Selection** — Tender greens sell fast, especially as weekend markets get busy.

**Check the Leaves** — Look for vibrant color, crisp texture, and no brown edges or yellowing.

**Ask About Washing** — Some farmers wash their greens; others prefer you do it at home. Either way, store them properly.

**Buy What’s in Season** — If you see greens that shouldn't be available yet (like certain lettuces in early March in cooler climates), ask your farmer about their growing method — they might be using season extension techniques like high tunnels or greenhouses.

## Storing Your Greens

Tender spring greens are delicate. Here's how to keep them fresh:

1. **Don't wash until ready to use** — Excess moisture causes greens to wilt faster.
2. **Use a reusable produce bag** — These let greens breathe better than plastic.
3. **Refrigerate immediately** — Most spring greens last 3-5 days when properly stored.
4. **Pat dry before cooking** — Dry greens wilt better and don't become watery in the pan.

## Spring Greens: Your First Step to Market Mastery

If you've never been to a farmers market, spring is the perfect time to start. The produce is beautiful, the weather is (usually) pleasant, and there's something almost ceremonial about buying the first spring greens of the year.

Start simple: grab a bunch of spinach, some spring onions, and a head of butter lettuce. That's everything you need for a week of delicious, seasonal eating.
